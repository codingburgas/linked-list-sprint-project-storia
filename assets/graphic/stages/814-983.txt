The period between 814 and 983 marks the continued expansion, cultural development, and challenges faced by the First Bulgarian Empire. During this time, Bulgaria reached its greatest territorial extent and saw the introduction of Christianity as the state religion.

814-831
Khan Omurtag, the son of Khan Krum, focused on consolidating and strengthening Bulgaria. He signed a 30-year peace treaty with the Byzantine Empire, allowing Bulgaria to stabilize its borders. Omurtag is known for extensive construction projects, including palaces, fortresses, and bridges. His rule strengthened central authority and established a strong administrative system.

831-836
Khan Malamir continued his predecessor’s policies, maintaining stability and securing the empire. He engaged in conflicts with the Byzantines but also saw internal development within Bulgaria.

836-852
Khan Presian expanded Bulgarian rule into Macedonia, bringing Slavic tribes under Bulgarian control. His reign laid the groundwork for cultural and religious developments that would occur under his successor.

852-889
Boris I converted to Christianity in 864 and established it as the state religion, which helped integrate Bulgaria into the European Christian world and strengthened ties with Byzantium. In 886, he welcomed the disciples of Saints Cyril and Methodius, promoting the use of the Slavic script and laying the foundation for Bulgarian cultural and literary achievements. Boris I later retired to a monastery, leaving the throne to his son Vladimir.

889-893
Vladimir attempted to restore paganism, which led to instability. In response, Boris I came out of retirement, removed Vladimir from power, and placed his younger son, Simeon, on the throne.

893-927
Simeon I transformed Bulgaria into a dominant power in the Balkans. He waged wars against Byzantium and expanded Bulgarian territory, even reaching the gates of Constantinople. His reign is known as the "Golden Age" due to advancements in culture, literature, and the establishment of Old Bulgarian as a literary language. He declared himself "Tsar" (Emperor), emphasizing Bulgaria’s independence and power.

927-969
Peter I signed a peace treaty with Byzantium, securing Bulgaria’s recognition as an empire. However, internal strife and external threats, particularly from the Kievan Rus and the Byzantine Empire, weakened Bulgaria. His rule saw the rise of the Bogomil heresy, a religious movement that opposed the established church and state authority.

969-977
During Boris II’s rule, Bulgaria suffered attacks from both the Byzantine Empire and the Kievan Rus under Sviatoslav. In 971, the Byzantines captured the Bulgarian capital, Preslav, and forced Boris II to surrender, marking the beginning of Byzantine dominance over Bulgaria.

977-991
After Boris II was captured, his brother Roman took over the resistance against Byzantine rule. However, his reign was weak, and Bulgaria continued to lose territory and influence.

976-983
During the late 10th century, four noble brothers from the Cometopuli dynasty—David, Moses, Aron, and Samuil—led a resistance movement against Byzantine rule. By 983, Samuil had emerged as the dominant leader, setting the stage for a resurgence of Bulgarian independence.

Conclusion
The period from 814 to 983 was one of great transformations for Bulgaria. Under rulers like Boris I and Simeon I, the country expanded territorially and culturally, experiencing its "Golden Age." However, after Simeon’s death, Bulgaria faced decline due to internal struggles and external threats. By the end of this period, the Cometopuli brothers were leading the fight to restore Bulgarian sovereignty, setting the stage for the reign of Tsar Samuil in the late 10th and early 11th centuries.
