Khan Omurtag (814-831): Consolidated Bulgaria after Krum’s death, signed a 30-year peace treaty with Byzantium, and undertook large building projects, strengthening central authority.

Khan Malamir (831-836): Maintained stability and continued his father Omurtag's policies; faced some conflicts with Byzantium, focusing on internal security.

Khan Presian (836-852): Expanded Bulgaria’s control over Macedonia, integrating Slavic tribes and strengthening Bulgaria’s regional influence.

Khan Boris I (852-889): Converted Bulgaria to Christianity in 864, aligning the country with Byzantium and introducing the Cyrillic alphabet, later retiring to a monastery.

Khan Vladimir (889-893): Attempted to reverse the Christianization of Bulgaria, but was deposed by his father Boris I, who restored Christianity.

Khan Simeon I (893-927): Known as Simeon the Great, he expanded Bulgaria’s territory, declared himself Tsar, and brought about a "Golden Age" of cultural and religious prosperity.

Tsar Peter I (927-969): Strengthened Bulgaria through a peace treaty with Byzantium but struggled with internal divisions, including the rise of the Bogomil heresy.

Tsar Boris II (969-977): His reign was marked by Byzantine incursions; he was eventually captured and forced to surrender to Byzantium in 971.

Tsar Roman (977-991): Led Bulgaria in a time of decline and continued to resist Byzantine domination, though his rule was largely ineffective.

Cometopuli Brothers (976-983): Led the resistance against Byzantium, with Samuil eventually becoming the dominant leader, laying the foundation for Bulgaria’s resurgence.
